Moving is one of the most stressful life events most people go through. And the reason it's so stressful isn't the boxes or the heavy furniture — it's the constant fear that you're forgetting something important.

You are.

Not because you're disorganized, but because there's no good master list anywhere that covers everything in the right order. This is that list.

Whether you're renting, buying, moving across town, or relocating to a new state, this checklist covers every step — organized by timeline so you know what to do and when to do it.

---

## 8 Weeks Before Your Move

The further out you start, the less you'll scramble at the end.

**Lock in your moving date**
Everything else on this list flows from your move date. Pick it, put it in writing, and protect it.

**Research movers or reserve a truck**
If you're hiring movers, 8 weeks out is not too early — especially for summer moves. Get at least three quotes. Verify they're licensed and insured. For a DIY move, reserve your truck or container now. Availability disappears fast.

**Start a moving binder or folder**
Keep every quote, receipt, contract, and confirmation in one place. You'll thank yourself later.

**Declutter before you pack**
Every item you don't move is one less thing to pack, haul, unpack, and find a home for. Go room by room. Donate, sell, or trash anything you haven't used in a year.

**Notify your employer**
If your move affects your commute, working hours, or tax situation (especially for a state-to-state move), give your HR department a heads up now.

## 6 Weeks Before Your Move

**Give notice to your landlord**
Most leases require 30–60 days written notice. Check yours. Miss this and you may owe an extra month's rent.

**Start collecting packing supplies**
Boxes, tape, bubble wrap, markers. Liquor stores and grocery stores often give away sturdy boxes for free.

**Research your new area**
If you're moving to a new city or state, start identifying:
- New doctors, dentists, and specialists
- New schools if you have kids
- Local grocery stores, pharmacies, and services
- Nearest DMV office

**Get school records if you have children**
Contact your current school and request transfer paperwork. Don't wait — schools can be slow.

**Review your insurance**
Contact your homeowners or renters insurance provider. Your current policy likely won't cover your new address from day one. Set up the new policy to start on move-in day.

## 4 Weeks Before Your Move

**File a change of address with USPS**
Go to usps.com and submit a change of address form. It costs $1.10 to verify your identity online. Mail forwarding starts within a few days and lasts up to 12 months — enough time to catch anything you miss.

**Start packing non-essentials**
Books, off-season clothes, décor, and anything you won't need in the next month. Label every box with the room it's going to and a brief description of contents.

**Transfer or cancel local memberships**
Gym memberships, local clubs, recreational programs. Some gyms have national networks — check before you cancel. Others require 30 days notice to avoid being charged.

**Contact utility companies**
Schedule disconnection at your current address and connection at your new one. Do this early — utility companies often need 1–2 weeks notice and appointment windows fill up.

**Utilities to contact:**
- Electric
- Gas
- Water/sewer
- Internet and cable
- Trash and recycling

**Collect important documents**
Birth certificates, Social Security cards, passports, medical records, tax returns, vehicle titles. Pack these separately and transport them yourself — don't let them go on the moving truck.

## 2 Weeks Before Your Move

**Confirm everything with your moving company**
Call to confirm the date, time, and address. Get it in writing. Ask about payment methods and what happens if there are delays.

**Begin the address change process**
This takes longer than people expect. Work through the list below over a few days.

### Complete Address Change Checklist

**Financial**
- [ ] Bank accounts (checking and savings)
- [ ] Credit cards
- [ ] Investment and retirement accounts (401k, IRA, brokerage)
- [ ] Loans (auto, student, personal, mortgage)
- [ ] PayPal, Venmo, Cash App

**Government**
- [ ] IRS (update at irs.gov or on your next tax return)
- [ ] Social Security Administration
- [ ] Voter registration (varies by state — search "[your new state] voter registration")
- [ ] Veterans Affairs (if applicable)

**Employer and income**
- [ ] Your HR department (for W-2 and payroll)
- [ ] Any freelance clients who send 1099s

**Insurance**
- [ ] Health insurance
- [ ] Auto insurance
- [ ] Life insurance
- [ ] Homeowners or renters insurance

**Subscriptions and deliveries**
- [ ] Amazon
- [ ] Any subscription boxes (HelloFresh, Chewy, etc.)
- [ ] Newspapers and magazines
- [ ] Online shopping accounts (Walmart, Target, etc.)

**Medical**
- [ ] Primary care doctor
- [ ] Dentist
- [ ] Specialists
- [ ] Pharmacy (request records transfer if switching)
- [ ] Health insurance member portal

**Other**
- [ ] Friends and family
- [ ] Professional licenses (if you hold any)
- [ ] Loyalty programs and rewards accounts

**Pack room by room**
Finish packing everything except what you'll need for the last few nights. Keep an "open first" box with essentials — toilet paper, soap, phone charger, a change of clothes, coffee supplies.

## Moving Week

**Confirm utilities at your new address are active**
Call and confirm. Don't assume because you scheduled it that it happened. Arriving to no electricity or no internet is a bad way to start.

**Do a final walkthrough of your old place**
Check every cabinet, closet, shelf, and drawer. Look behind doors, inside appliances, and in the garage. Don't forget the attic, basement, or storage unit if you have one.

**Document the condition of your old home**
Take time-stamped photos and video of every room before you leave. If you're renting, this protects your security deposit. If you're buying, it's part of your sale records.

**Defrost the freezer**
If you're moving the refrigerator, unplug it 24 hours before the move and leave the doors open.

**Be home or have someone there for the movers**
Don't leave movers alone in your home. Be present to direct them, answer questions, and do a final count of items before they leave.

**Do a walkthrough of your new home before unloading**
Check that utilities work, identify any existing damage, and decide where large furniture goes before it gets carried inside.

## Moving Day

**Keep these with you, not on the truck:**
- Important documents folder
- Medications
- Valuables and jewelry
- Laptops and devices
- Your "open first" box
- Cash or payment for movers

**Inspect and sign carefully**
Before movers leave, walk through and note any damage on the bill of lading. Signing without noting damage makes it harder to file a claim later.

**Change the locks**
If you're moving into a home you purchased, change the locks on your first day. You don't know how many copies of the key are floating around.

## First Week After Your Move

**Set up the essentials first**
Bed, bathroom, and kitchen. Get these functional before anything else. Sleeping well and being able to cook basic meals makes the rest of unpacking far less exhausting.

**Update your driver's license and vehicle registration**
This is one of the most commonly forgotten tasks — and one of the most time-sensitive. Most states require you to update your license within 30–60 days of moving. Vehicle registration deadlines vary by state.

**What you'll need at the DMV:**
- Proof of new address (utility bill or lease agreement)
- Current driver's license
- Vehicle title (for registration transfer)
- Proof of insurance

**Register to vote at your new address**
If you moved to a new county or state, your voter registration doesn't automatically transfer. Check your new state's deadlines — many states require registration at least 15–30 days before an election.

**File for a homestead exemption if you bought**
If you purchased your home and plan to use it as your primary residence, a homestead exemption can reduce your property tax. Most counties have a deadline — often within 30–90 days of your purchase date. Check with your county assessor.

**Find a new doctor and dentist**
Don't wait until you need one. It can take weeks to get a new patient appointment. Call now while things are calm.

## 30 Days After Your Move

By now the boxes should be mostly unpacked and the chaos has settled. This is when people relax — and forget the last few items.

**Check your mail forwarding**
Is forwarded mail arriving? If not, contact USPS. Also check whether anything critical is still going to your old address that you haven't updated.

**File for in-state tuition if you're a student**
If you moved to a new state for school, the clock on residency requirements for in-state tuition starts on your move-in date. Check your school's requirements and file any paperwork promptly.

**Update your professional licenses**
If you hold any state-issued professional licenses — real estate, nursing, CPA, contractor, etc. — most states require you to update your address or transfer your license within a set timeframe.

**Review your budget for the new area**
Your cost of living may have changed. New commute costs, property taxes, local taxes, utility rates, and service costs all affect your monthly expenses. Update your budget before any surprises hit.

**Handle any remaining address updates**
Go back through your address change checklist and verify everything was updated. Pull your credit report and look for any addresses that still show your old location.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How far in advance should I start planning a move?**
For a local move, 4–6 weeks is sufficient if you're organized. For a long-distance or cross-country move, start 8–12 weeks out. The earlier you start, the more options you have for movers, truck rentals, and utility scheduling.

**What's the most important thing to do immediately after moving?**
Update your driver's license and vehicle registration. Most states have a 30–60 day deadline, and it's the task people most commonly delay and then get fined for.

**How do I change my address with the IRS?**
The easiest way is to include your new address on your next tax return. You can also file Form 8822 (Change of Address) with the IRS directly at irs.gov. This is important if you're expecting a tax refund or any IRS correspondence.

**Do I need to register to vote again after moving?**
If you moved within the same county, you may just need to update your registration. If you moved to a new county or state, you'll need to register again. Do this as soon as possible — many states have registration deadlines 15–30 days before elections.

**What's the difference between a local and a long-distance move?**
The industry definition is 100 miles. Moves under 100 miles are typically charged by the hour. Moves over 100 miles are usually priced by weight and distance. Long-distance moves require more advance planning, especially for movers.

**How do I avoid losing my security deposit?**
Document the condition of the unit before you move in (photos and video with timestamps) and again when you move out. Give proper written notice according to your lease. Clean thoroughly. Address any minor repairs that are your responsibility under the lease.
